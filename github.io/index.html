📢 Content warning: This study contains examples of harmful language

Overview

Jailbreak attacks are dangerous not only when they are effective, but when they are also difficult to detect. Existing jailbreak methods often face a fundamental trade-off: they either hide malicious intent at the cost of unnatural language, or preserve fluent language while exposing harmful semantics.

StegoAttack resolves this tension with linguistic steganography. It embeds a harmful query inside a benign, semantically coherent paragraph, so the carrier text remains natural while the hidden payload conceals the malicious intent.

In experiments on four state-of-the-art safety-aligned LLMs, including GPT-5 and Gemini-3, StegoAttack achieves an average ASR of 95.50% and drops by less than 27.00% under external detectors, outperforming eight leading jailbreak baselines.

Motivation

As safety-aligned LLMs become more robust, a jailbreak is no longer threatening only because it can bypass refusal. The more serious risk is stealth: whether the attack can avoid both model-side safeguards and external safety detectors while still producing harmful behavior.

Existing jailbreaks usually optimize only one side of this problem. Linguistic-stealth methods can make prompts look fluent and human-like, but the harmful intent often remains semantically exposed. Semantic-stealth methods can obscure toxic content through encoding or fragmentation, but the resulting text often looks unnatural and easy to flag.

Research question: Can a jailbreak conceal malicious intent while preserving natural language fluency, and can that concealment persist across both the input and the model response?

StegoAttack is motivated by this gap. It borrows the principle of steganography: hide the payload in plain sight inside a benign carrier paragraph. This lets the overt text remain coherent while the hidden channel carries the adversarial objective.

Method

At a high level, StegoAttack builds an end-to-end steganographic attack pipeline. It first embeds a hidden query into a fluent benign paragraph through masked regeneration, then introduces steganographic in-context examples that carry latent behavioral patterns, and finally uses a structured prompt template to guide extraction, response generation, and response concealment.

Experiments

The main evaluation compares StegoAttack with eight jailbreak methods on four safety-aligned target models: Gemini-3, GPT-5, DeepSeek-V3.2, and Qwen3-max. The reported metrics include ASR, harmfulness score, and relatedness score.

  • StegoAttack achieves the highest average ASR in the main comparison.
  • The paper reports 100.00% ASR on Gemini-3 and 82.67% ASR on GPT-5.
  • Steganographic ICL improves GPT-5 ASR by 40.67 percentage points and harmfulness score by 1.50 in the ablation study.
  • Under external guards, StegoAttack maintains robustness with only a 26.33% ASR reduction in the reported stealth evaluation.

Resources

The repository is organized around the paper pipeline:

  • Hidden: steganographic carrier generation.
  • Attack: template-based attack stage.
  • Evaluation: linguistic stealth and semantic harmfulness metrics.
  • Data: normalized benchmark inputs.
  • Baselines: baseline papers, links, and migrated prompt renderers where available.

Ethics and Limitations

The paper frames StegoAttack as a security evaluation study intended to motivate stronger safeguards and more rigorous detector research. It also notes that future model capabilities may make more covert steganographic channels possible, including techniques that hide information across multiple words or sub-word fragments.
